Sound familiar?

Nobody warns you about the in-between years.

The decade where every choice feels permanent, everyone has opinions, and "you're doing great!" somehow makes it worse. Women in these years aren't working on one thing — they're forming new roles, often several at once, while still seeking an identity that's fully their own. Therapy here makes room for all of it.

  • Everyone thinks you're doing great. You're mostly doing tired.
  • Sunday nights feel like a countdown. Monday mornings, a performance.
  • Your group chat gets the funny version. Nobody gets the real one.
  • Every decision — job, city, relationship — feels irreversible.
  • You compare your inside to everyone else's highlight reel and lose.
  • You keep saying "after this busy season." It's been four seasons.

Becoming yourself

Identity, boundaries, relationships, the gap between the life you built and the one you want. We sort what's actually yours from what you inherited — and build from there.

New roles, all at once

Partner, professional, daughter, friend, maybe someday mother — each one arriving with its own job description. We work on holding the roles without losing the person underneath them.

A place for exploration

You don't need a crisis to come. Some of the best work happens when nothing is "wrong" — you're deciding who you're becoming, and it helps to think out loud with someone trained to listen for you in it.

Burnout & the pressure to perform

Perfectionism, imposter feelings, running on fumes at 24. This isn't a pep talk or productivity coaching — it's clinical care for the anxiety and self-worth machinery underneath.

How treatment works

Evidence-based, in plain words.

Catch the spiral (CBT)

Anxiety runs on predictable thought loops — catastrophizing, mind-reading, "what if." We learn to catch them mid-spin and answer back, until your brain stops taking every worry's word for it.

Calm the body (DBT skills)

Racing heart, tight chest, the stomach that knots before work. You'll get concrete regulation skills that work in the moment — in a hallway, an office, a car.

Live anyway (ACT)

The goal isn't a life with zero anxiety — it's a life anxiety doesn't run. We shrink avoidance step by step, so the things you've been putting off stop waiting on a "someday" that never comes.

Anxiety shows up differently at different ages — school-shaped for teens, 3am-shaped for new moms, expectation-shaped for first-gen families. The treatment bends to fit.

When it shows up as anxiety

Anxiety doesn't always look anxious.

Sometimes it looks like straight A's, a full calendar, and a mind that never stops grading you. High-functioning doesn't mean fine.

  • Your brain replays today and rehearses tomorrow, every night.
  • Stomach aches, headaches, a chest that never fully unclenches.
  • You've gotten very good at avoiding the things that spike it.
  • Every text you send gets reread. Every "we need to talk" is a verdict.
  • People say "you have nothing to worry about." It doesn't help.
  • You're exhausted from performing calm all day.
Grief & loss

Grief therapy for the losses that rearrange everything.

Grief rarely looks the way anyone expects. A grandparent, a parent, a friend — and the losses no one sends cards for: divorce, a move, a friendship that ended, a future that changed shape. If it rearranged your world, it counts here.

From 2020–2026 I ran grief groups and led crisis response as a school counselor in the West Windsor–Plainsboro schools — walking students and families through sudden losses, long illnesses, and the seasons after. Grief-informed care isn't a specialty I added; it's the core of how I was trained by the work itself. In my room, grief is not a problem to fix. It's a weight we learn to carry differently.

Grief & loss is a named lane of this practice — no stages checklist, no timeline. For grieving teens and families, start with the teen therapy page.

Questions

Fair questions, honest answers.

How long until therapy helps?

Honestly: it varies. Most people learn their first usable skills within the first few sessions — things that take the edge off a spiral that same week. Deeper change, like shrinking avoidance and rewiring old thought habits, builds over months. We check progress together as we go, and the pace is always yours.

Do I need a diagnosis to start?

No. You don't need a label, a referral, or proof that it's "bad enough." If worry is taking up more of your life than you want it to, that's reason enough. If a formal diagnosis becomes useful — for insurance or school accommodations — we'll handle it together, transparently.

What if talking about it makes it worse?

A fair fear — and a common one. Therapy here isn't forced exposure or reliving everything at once. We go at your pace, build calming skills before we approach the hard parts, and you always have the wheel. Most people find that naming the worry out loud shrinks it more than silence ever did.
